The records are already public. A tenant facing eviction can legally read their building's violation history — if they can find the right dataset, filter forty million rows, and decode the column names. That gap is the whole problem.

Every CivicByte tool collapses that gap to one search box: type an address or a landlord's name, get the city's own records back in plain language, with the source dataset named and linked so anyone can check our work.

This page is new and so are we — CivicByte launched in June 2026. As real outcomes happen — a record used in housing court, a budget cited at a hearing — we'll document them here, verified with the people involved, and never invented. If one of these tools helped you, tell us.

The tools need no accounts and keep none. Your searches go straight from your browser to the city's public APIs — they never pass through a CivicByte server. What we do count: aggregate, non-identifying page views, so we know which tools get used, and field reports people send us directly.

Anything we ever publish here as a case, hearing, or filing will be verified with the person involved first. The full measurement setup is described in our privacy note.
